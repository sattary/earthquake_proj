

# **A Dynamic, Data-Driven Earthquake Forecast for Central Iran: A Hybrid Machine Learning and Seismicity-Based Approach**

## **1.0 Introduction: Beyond Static Hazard Assessment**

### **1.1 The Critical Limitations of Static Seismic Hazard Maps**

For decades, the cornerstone of seismic risk assessment has been the Probabilistic Seismic Hazard Analysis (PSHA), which culminates in static hazard maps. These maps, exemplified by global efforts like the Global Seismic Hazard Assessment Program (GSHAP), typically depict the peak ground acceleration with a certain probability of being exceeded over a long time horizon, such as 10% in 50 years.1 While foundational for developing building codes and long-term planning, this approach is predicated on a critical and increasingly challenged assumption: that earthquake occurrence is a stationary, time-independent Poisson process. This simplification fundamentally ignores the well-documented temporal and spatial clustering of seismic events.  
The most significant deficiency of static PSHA arises from the profound mismatch between the length of our observational records and the duration of seismic cycles.3 Instrumental and historical earthquake catalogs, even those spanning a century, represent a mere snapshot of geological time. In intraplate regions like central Iran, where deformation rates are slow and major earthquake recurrence intervals can be thousands of years, this short record is particularly problematic. It can lead to a dangerously distorted view of seismic hazard, creating misleading "bull's-eyes" of high risk around the sites of recent large earthquakes while underestimating the hazard in areas of apparent seismic quiescence.3 These quiescent zones may not be safe, but rather may be late in their seismic cycle and approaching a future rupture. Furthermore, the visual communication of static maps can inadvertently misrepresent risk through suboptimal use of color palettes and data classification schemes that fail to convey the continuous and uncertain nature of the hazard.5

### **1.2 The Imperative for Dynamic, Time-Dependent Forecasting**

The clear time-dependency of seismicity, evident in the evolution of earthquake sequences with distinct mainshocks, aftershocks, and swarms, necessitates a paradigm shift from static assessment to dynamic forecasting.7 The probability of an earthquake at a given location is not constant; it increases dramatically in the aftermath of a nearby event and decays over time. This understanding has given rise to the field of Operational Earthquake Forecasting (OEF), defined as the authoritative gathering and dissemination of time-dependent seismic hazard information to help communities prepare for potentially destructive earthquakes.10

### **1.3 A Novel Hybrid Approach for Central Iran**

This project proposes the development of a novel hybrid forecasting model for central Iran, a region of significant but diffuse intraplate seismicity. The model is designed to overcome the limitations of both static PSHA and conventional time-dependent models by synergistically integrating data-driven machine learning with established principles of statistical seismology. The innovation of this approach lies in its two-phase architecture. First, a machine learning model will be trained to produce a static, time-independent map of spatial seismic susceptibility. This map will not be based on seismicity alone, but on fundamental, long-term geophysical properties of the Earth's crust that predispose certain areas to failure. Second, this physically-grounded spatial map will be ingested as a spatially-variable background rate into a temporal forecasting model, the Epidemic-Type Aftershock Sequence (ETAS) model.  
This hybrid structure directly addresses a principal weakness of many standard ETAS implementations, which often assume a spatially uniform or crudely smoothed background seismicity rate, detached from underlying geology.12 By informing the background rate with geophysical data, the proposed model aims to create a more physically realistic and powerful forecast. This architecture is not merely a technical integration of two algorithms; it represents a conceptual framework that explicitly couples the two distinct physical and temporal scales governing earthquake occurrence. The machine learning component models the slow, geologic processes of stress accumulation and strain localization over millennia, which determine  
*where* the crust is inherently weak. The ETAS component models the rapid, dynamic process of stress transfer and triggering following an earthquake, which governs *when* subsequent events are most likely to occur in the short term. This approach bridges the gap between long-term crustal mechanics and short-term earthquake statistics, yielding a more holistic physical model of the seismogenic process.

## **2.0 Tectonic Setting and Datasets for Central Iran**

### **2.1 Seismotectonic Framework of the Study Area**

The tectonic environment of Iran is dominated by the ongoing collision between the Arabian and Eurasian plates, which converge at a rate of approximately 2.1-2.5 cm per year.13 This convergence has formed the Alpide-Himalayan seismic belt, making the Iranian plateau one of the most seismically active continental regions in the world.13 The plateau is not a monolithic block but is composed of several distinct seismotectonic provinces, each with unique characteristics. These include the highly active Zagros fold-and-thrust belt in the southwest, the Alborz mountain range in the north, and the Central Iran block, which is the focus of this study.13  
The Central Iran block behaves as a relatively rigid microplate, caught between the more intensely deforming zones of the Zagros and Alborz. Its seismicity is characterized as intraplate, with earthquakes that are more scattered and less frequent than in the Zagros belt but are often of large magnitude (M\>6.0) and occur at shallow depths, posing a significant hazard to population centers.16 Tectonic strain across this block is accommodated primarily along a network of major, well-defined, and often long and linear fault systems.13 Prominent among these are the north-south trending, right-lateral strike-slip Nayband and Gowk fault systems, which bound the rigid Lut block, and major east-west trending left-lateral structures such as the Doruneh fault.15 This tectonic regime, with distributed seismicity controlled by major crustal structures, makes central Iran an ideal natural laboratory for developing and testing a forecasting model designed to capture the interplay between long-term geological controls and short-term earthquake triggering.

### **2.2 Integrated Geophysical and Seismological Datasets**

The proposed model is founded on the integration of three complementary, high-quality datasets that together provide a comprehensive picture of the crustal system. The power of the modeling approach stems from a physical framework that views these datasets as a causal sequence. The kinematic data represents the *forcing function*. the tectonic loads being applied to the system. The structural data describes the *physical properties* of the system being loaded. Finally, the seismicity catalog is the *observed response*. the record of where and when the system has failed under these loads. This framing elevates the machine learning component beyond simple statistical correlation, tasking it with learning an approximation of the physical transfer function that maps tectonic forcing and crustal properties to the likelihood of seismic failure.  
**Structural Data:** A high-resolution 3D tomographic model of crustal shear wave velocity (Vs‚Äč) will be utilized. Vs‚Äč is a fundamental geophysical parameter that serves as a direct proxy for the mechanical properties of the crust, such as rigidity and shear modulus. Regions with low Vs‚Äč can indicate weaker, more fractured, or thermally anomalous crust that is more susceptible to failure, whereas high Vs‚Äč zones often correspond to strong, rigid crustal blocks that can accumulate significant stress.20  
**Kinematic Data:** A comprehensive suite of crustal deformation measurements will be used to quantify the tectonic forces loading the region. This includes principal stress orientations derived from inversions of numerous earthquake focal mechanisms and a dense grid of crustal strain rates calculated from a regional network of Global Positioning System (GPS) stations.13 Together, these data define the contemporary stress and strain field to which the crust must respond, indicating both the direction and magnitude of tectonic loading.24  
**Seismicity Data:** A homogenized and declustered earthquake catalog for the study region, spanning approximately 85 years, will form the observational basis of the model. This dataset provides the ground truth for crustal failure. It will be used in two critical ways: first, to define the target variable for the spatial machine learning model (i.e., the locations of past earthquakes), and second, as the primary input for the temporal ETAS model, providing the timing, location, and magnitude of all past events that can trigger subsequent seismicity.16

## **3.0 A Two-Phase Hybrid Forecasting Methodology**

### **3.1 Phase 1: Machine Learning for Spatial Susceptibility (őľ(x,y))**

The first phase of the methodology aims to create a static, time-independent map of earthquake susceptibility based on the long-term physical properties of the crust. This map will serve as the background rate, őľ(x,y), for the time-dependent model in Phase 2\.  
**Grid Discretization and Feature Engineering:** The study area will be discretized into a uniform grid of cells, for example, with a resolution of 0.1¬į x 0.1¬į. For each cell, a vector of quantitative features will be engineered from the structural and kinematic datasets. This step is critical for translating our physical understanding of seismogenesis into a format that a machine learning algorithm can interpret.28 The features are designed to capture key physical attributes believed to control the location of earthquakes, as detailed in Table 2\.

**Target Variable Formulation:** To train the model in a supervised manner, a binary target variable (y) will be formulated. Grid cells that contain the epicenter of one or more earthquakes from the historical catalog (above a carefully determined magnitude of completeness, e.g., M‚Č•4.0) will be labeled as hazardous (y=1). All other grid cells will be labeled as non-hazardous (y=0). This formulation transforms the problem of spatial hazard mapping into a well-posed binary classification task.30  
**Model Selection and Training:** An Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) classifier will be employed for this task. XGBoost is a powerful, tree-based ensemble algorithm renowned for its high predictive performance, computational efficiency, and inherent regularization to prevent overfitting.32 Its effectiveness has been demonstrated in a variety of geophysical hazard mapping applications, including landslide susceptibility and seismic hazard assessment.32 The model will be trained using the engineered feature vectors (  
X) as input and the binary hazardous/non-hazardous labels (y) as the target. A rigorous cross-validation scheme will be used to tune the model's hyperparameters and to provide an unbiased estimate of its generalization performance.   
**Output Generation:** Once trained, the XGBoost model will be applied to every grid cell across the entire study area to predict the probability of that cell belonging to the "Hazardous" class, P(y=1). The resulting continuous field of probabilities, ranging from 0 to 1, represents the long-term, geologically controlled likelihood of an earthquake occurring at any given location. This map will be normalized to serve as the spatially-variable, time-independent background seismicity rate, őľ(x,y), for the next phase.

### **3.2 Phase 2: Temporal Evolution with a Spatially-Informed ETAS Model**

The second phase of the model introduces the dimension of time by modeling the well-known phenomenon of earthquake clustering, where each earthquake can trigger a sequence of aftershocks.  
**ETAS Framework:** The Epidemic-Type Aftershock Sequence (ETAS) model is a stochastic point-process model that has become a benchmark in statistical seismology for describing and forecasting seismicity.36 The model defines the conditional intensity (the expected rate of earthquakes) at any time t and location (x,y), given the history of past earthquakes Ht‚Äč, as a sum of two components: a stationary background rate and a triggered component. The full intensity is given by:

Each term has a clear physical interpretation:

* őľ(x,y) is the background rate of seismicity, representing earthquakes that are not triggered by preceding events in the catalog but are driven by long-term tectonic loading.  
* The summation term represents the contribution from all past earthquakes that occurred at time ti‚Äč and location (xi‚Äč,yi‚Äč) with magnitude Mi‚Äč.

**The Hybrid Integration:** The central innovation of this project is the method of defining the background rate őľ(x,y). Instead of assuming a constant or a simple smoothed function for őľ, this project will directly substitute the detailed, physically-constrained susceptibility map generated by the machine learning model in Phase 1\.10 This crucial step ensures that the background probability of an earthquake is not an abstract statistical construct but is directly tied to the underlying geological and geophysical properties of the crust.  
**Parameter Estimation and Dynamic Forecast Generation:** With the background rate őľ(x,y) fixed as a spatial prior, the remaining parameters of the ETAS model (e.g., A,őĪ,c,p,K and parameters of the spatial kernel) will be estimated by fitting the model to the entire 85-year seismicity catalog using a robust statistical method such as the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.12 Once all parameters are determined, the full hybrid model becomes operational. After any new earthquake is recorded in the region, the conditional intensity  
őĽ(t,x,y‚ą£Ht‚Äč) across the entire grid will be instantaneously recalculated. This update will show a sharp increase in earthquake probability in the vicinity of the new event, which will then gradually decay over subsequent hours, days, and weeks according to Omori's Law. This process generates a continuously evolving, dynamic map of earthquake probability, a true seismic forecast.

## **4.0 Anticipated Outcomes and Scientific Contributions**

### **4.1 The Dynamic Earthquake Forecast Map**

The primary deliverable of this research will be a dynamic earthquake forecast map for central Iran. This will be a significant advancement over any existing static hazard product for the region. The forecast will be visualized through a near-real-time dashboard, displaying the probability of an earthquake (e.g., M‚Č•4.0) occurring within each grid cell over a specified future time window (e.g., the next 24 hours, 7 days, or 30 days).43 A key feature of this system is its dynamic nature; the map will be designed to update automatically following the detection of any new significant seismic event in the region, providing an evolving picture of seismic hazard that reflects the current state of earthquake clustering and stress transfer.43 This stands in stark contrast to existing static hazard maps, which are updated infrequently (on the order of years or decades) and cannot respond to short-term changes in seismicity.47

### **4.2 Insights into Seismogenic Processes**

Beyond the operational forecast product, this project is designed to yield fundamental scientific insights into the processes that govern where and when earthquakes occur in a continental interior.  
**Quantifying Geophysical Controls on Seismicity:** A crucial scientific outcome will be a quantitative, physically interpretable model of the factors controlling earthquake locations in central Iran. The "black box" nature of many machine learning models is a common criticism, but this project will overcome this by employing state-of-the-art eXplainable AI (XAI) techniques, specifically SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations).50 SHAP analysis will be applied to the trained XGBoost model from Phase 1 to precisely quantify the contribution of each input geophysical feature, such as crustal rigidity (Vs‚Äč), proximity to crustal weaknesses (Vs‚Äč gradient), and the rate of tectonic loading (strain rate) to the model's final prediction of seismic susceptibility for every location on the map.53 This provides a powerful tool for hypothesis testing, allowing us to move beyond simple correlation to understand the relative importance and influence of different physical processes in a complex tectonic setting.56  
**Decomposition of Seismic Hazard:** The hybrid model's architecture naturally allows for the decomposition of seismic hazard into two physically distinct components: (1) a static, background component driven by the slow, multi-millennial process of geological loading and strain accumulation, captured by the machine learning model; and (2) a dynamic, triggered component driven by the rapid process of stress transfer from recent earthquakes, captured by the ETAS model. This formal separation will enable a detailed analysis of the relative contributions of background seismicity versus aftershock clustering to the total hazard across the region, offering fundamental insights into the earthquake cycle and the interplay between long-term and short-term seismic behavior.58  
The application of SHAP analysis is not merely to rank features by importance, but to uncover the complex, non-linear relationships and interactions between them that govern crustal failure. While a traditional statistical analysis might reveal a simple positive correlation, such as higher strain rates leading to more earthquakes, the physical reality is more nuanced. The XGBoost algorithm is capable of learning these high-order interaction effects, and SHAP dependence plots can visualize them.52 For instance, a potential discovery could be that high strain rates only significantly increase seismic susceptibility in regions of *intermediate* shear wave velocity that is, in crust that is neither too strong to resist failure nor too weak and ductile to accumulate significant elastic strain. Uncovering such a physically-plausible, non-linear relationship would constitute a significant new scientific insight into the specific rheological and mechanical conditions that promote intraplate earthquakes, an outcome made possible only by the powerful combination of a sophisticated machine learning model and advanced XAI interpretation techniques.

## **5.0 Advanced Methodological Considerations and Inherent Limitations**

### **5.1 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) for Spatial Modeling**

As an advanced alternative to the XGBoost model in Phase 1, this project will also explore the use of a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN). PINNs are a class of deep learning models that embed physical laws directly into the learning process, typically by including the residual of a governing partial differential equation (PDE) as a regularization term in the network's loss function.61 This forces the network's output to be consistent not only with the training data but also with the underlying physics of the system.65  
While there is no single, universally accepted PDE for long-term earthquake potential, a strong physical constraint can be imposed based on the nature of geological and geophysical fields: they are generally spatially continuous and smooth, not noisy or pixelated.67 Abrupt changes occur at faults, but the broader hazard field should reflect the continuous nature of stress and strain accumulation. To enforce this, a *spatial smoothness regularizer* will be implemented in the PINN's loss function.68 The total loss function will take the form:

 The hyperparameter őĽ controls the trade-off between fitting the sparse earthquake data and enforcing the physical constraint of smoothness.68 This approach is expected to yield more geologically realistic susceptibility maps that generalize better, particularly in regions where earthquake data is sparse, by preventing the model from overfitting to the specific locations of known events.71

### **5.2 Acknowledged Limitations and Assumptions**

**Earthquake Catalog Quality:** The quality of the earthquake catalog is the most significant potential limitation of this study. An 85-year record is short compared to the recurrence times of major faults in the region, providing only a limited sample of the long-term seismic process.3 More critically, the catalog is subject to incompleteness. The magnitude of completeness (  
Mc‚Äč) the level above which all events are reliably detected is not constant. It varies spatially with network density and has improved over time with technological advancements.73 Most importantly for this study, Mc‚Äč increases dramatically in the immediate aftermath of a large mainshock due to waveform overlap and elevated background noise, a phenomenon known as short-term aftershock incompleteness (STAI).10 Failing to account for STAI can severely bias the estimation of ETAS parameters, leading to an underestimation of aftershock productivity and inaccurate forecasts. Therefore, a mandatory prerequisite for this research will be a rigorous, spatially and temporally varying analysis of Mc‚Äč to ensure that only complete portions of the catalog are used for model training and validation.76  
**The Stationarity Assumption:** The Phase 1 model relies on the assumption that the geophysical fields used as features (Vs‚Äč, stress, strain) are stationary, or unchanging, over the 85-year observational period of the seismicity catalog. For long-term geological processes, this is a standard and necessary assumption in geophysics.77 However, it is an idealization. On decadal timescales, there may be subtle changes in these fields, and anthropogenic climate change may induce non-stationary loads on the crust through processes like changing groundwater levels or isostatic adjustments.79 This proposal acknowledges this assumption and its potential to introduce second-order uncertainty into the model.  
**Forecasting vs. Prediction:** It is imperative to be precise with terminology. The model developed in this project will produce a probabilistic *forecast*, not a deterministic *prediction*. It will quantify the time-dependent probability of future earthquakes occurring within a given region and time window. It will not, however, specify the exact time, location, and magnitude of a specific impending earthquake. Such a deterministic prediction is widely considered to be scientifically impossible at present, and this project makes no claim to achieve it.6

## **6.0 Conclusion: Advancing Probabilistic Seismic Hazard Analysis**

### **6.1 Summary of Innovation and Significance**

primary contribution is the development of a novel, physically-grounded, dynamic earthquake forecasting framework tailored for the complex intraplate setting of central Iran. By seamlessly integrating long-term geological and geophysical constraints via machine learning with the short-term clustering dynamics of seismicity via the ETAS model, the proposed hybrid approach represents a substantial evolution beyond both traditional static PSHA and standard ETAS models. The methodology provides a robust and transferable template for developing next-generation hazard and risk models that can be adapted to other seismically active regions worldwide, moving the field closer to the goal of operational earthquake forecasting.

### **6.2 Broader Societal Implications**

The successful development and validation of a dynamic earthquake forecast model for central Iran holds the potential to transform regional disaster risk management. An operational version of this system could provide actionable, short-term intelligence to civil protection agencies, emergency managers, and the public. This information could be used to guide decisions on heightening public awareness, pre-positioning emergency response resources, and implementing short-term mitigation actions for critical infrastructure in periods of elevated seismic hazard.82  
However, the transition from a research model to an operational public-facing forecast is fraught with significant societal challenges.84 An effective forecast is a socio-technical system, where the societal component is as critical as the technical one.86 The probabilistic nature of forecasts can be easily misinterpreted, and in the high-stakes environment of earthquake risk, misinformation and false predictions can spread rapidly, potentially causing undue panic or, conversely, a "cry wolf" effect that erodes public trust.11  
Therefore, a crucial element of this project's long-term vision is the responsible stewardship of its scientific output. This includes a commitment to future collaboration with social scientists, risk communication experts, and public authorities. The goal of this collaboration would be to co-develop clear, effective, and responsible communication strategies and visualization tools for the dynamic forecast products. This ensures that the scientific advancements are not only technically sound and accurate but are also translated into information that is understandable, useful, and safe for public consumption, ultimately enhancing community resilience in the face of seismic threats.
